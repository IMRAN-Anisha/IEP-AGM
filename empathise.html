I. Stage 01 · Listening & learning

Understanding Country,
people and power.

Before designing anything, we listened. We mapped the political, economic, social, technological, legal and environmental forces that shape life on Lama Lama Country, and the people whose decisions, knowledge and care make our work possible.

01 PESTLE Analysis

Six lenses on a complex system.

Desktop studies using the PESTLE framework helped identify the political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental factors influencing the project context. These studies highlighted how energy access, environmental conditions, regulatory requirements, and remote infrastructure constraints shape the design process and affect implementation in Lama Lama Country (Queensland Competition Authority, 2025; Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water, 2024; WorkSafe Queensland, 2020; Bureau of Meteorology & CSIRO, 2019). Considering these broader factors alongside stakeholder needs helped ensure the proposed design remained practical, culturally appropriate, and suitable for Rangers as the primary users.

P Political
  • Lama Lama Country relies on remote stand-alone power systems (SAPS) rather than the main electricity grid, limiting access to large-scale energy infrastructure and support services (Australian Energy Market Commission [AEMC], 2019; Ergon Energy, 2023a).
  • Existing systems in Port Stewart are ageing and expensive to maintain, with remote communities often responsible for fuel, transport, and repair costs (Australian Government, 2024; Mahmud & Roy, 2025).
  • Queensland energy subsidies such as the Uniform Tariff Policy mainly support grid-connected customers, meaning off-grid communities like Lama Lama receive less long-term assistance for ongoing operational costs (Queensland Competition Authority [QCA], 2025).
  • Previous programs such as Bushlight demonstrated that remote renewable systems can succeed when governments, technical organisations, and communities work together through long-term support and local training (Centre for Appropriate Technology [CfAT], 2008).
  • As a result, any monitoring or fault diagnosis system designed for Lama Lama Country should be low-cost, durable, easy to maintain locally, and capable of operating independently in remote conditions.
E Economic
  • Port Stewart’s energy systems are designed around affordability, reliability, and ease of local use due to the high costs associated with remote infrastructure and maintenance (Mahmud & Roy, 2025).
  • Existing Bushlight Energy Management Units (EMUs) use simple displays such as lights, meters, and timers to help residents monitor energy availability and manage battery use without extensive technical training (Centre for Appropriate Technology [CfAT], 2008; Martire, 2020).
  • Remote communities face high transport and repair costs because replacement components and qualified technicians must often travel long distances to reach Port Stewart (Mahmud & Roy, 2025).
  • Limited internet access outside Ranger bases also restricts the practicality of monitoring systems that rely on constant online connectivity (Engineers Without Borders Australia [EWB], 2026d).
  • As a result, monitoring and fault diagnosis systems for Lama Lama Country should be low-cost, durable, simple to maintain locally, and capable of operating with minimal training and internet dependence
S Social
  • Reliable energy is essential for communication, healthcare, education, employment, and everyday wellbeing within Lama Lama Country, meaning power failures can significantly affect community life beyond the energy system itself (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2026; Centre for Appropriate Technology [CfAT], n.d.).
  • Lama Lama Country is deeply connected to land, waters, and Sea Country, so new technologies must align with cultural values and community priorities (Lama Lama Organisation, 2021a).
  • Co-designing solutions with Elders, Rangers, and community members helps ensure monitoring systems are practical, culturally appropriate, and suited to local environmental conditions such as cyclones, flooding, and wet-season isolation (Engineers Without Borders Australia [EWB], 2026d).
  • Remote communities often rely on visiting technicians for maintenance, creating delays and additional costs when faults occur (Heard et al., 2025). Supporting local capability and simple maintenance can therefore improve long-term energy reliability and community independence.
  • Monitoring systems should also respect Indigenous Data Sovereignty by ensuring energy data remains controlled and managed in ways that benefit the Lama Lama community (National Indigenous Australians Agency, 2024).
  • As a result, the project solution should be easy to use, locally maintainable, culturally respectful, and designed to strengthen community autonomy while supporting care for Country.
T Technological
  • Lama Lama Country already uses off-grid renewable energy infrastructure at Port Stewart, including Bushlight solar systems with photovoltaic (PV) arrays, batteries, inverters, charge controllers, and Energy Management Units (EMUs) (Centre for Appropriate Technology [CfAT], 2008).
  • These systems were originally developed through community energy planning, showing the importance of designing technology around local needs and conditions (CfAT, 2008).
  • Maintaining remote energy systems is challenging because technical support, replacement parts, and qualified electricians are often located far from the community (Heard et al., 2025; Mahmud & Roy, 2025).
  • Undetected faults in solar PV systems can reduce energy generation, shorten equipment lifespan, and create safety risks, making early fault detection essential for reliable operation (El-Banby et al., 2023; Venkatakrishnan et al., 2023).
  • Existing research shows that effective remote monitoring systems commonly use continuous electrical monitoring and automated alerts to identify abnormal system behaviour early (El-Banby et al., 2023).
  • As a result, monitoring solutions for Lama Lama Country should be durable, low-complexity, easy to operate locally, and capable of providing early warnings for faults requiring maintenance or technical support.
L Legal
  • Energy monitoring systems on Lama Lama Country must comply with Queensland electrical safety and work health and safety legislation to ensure systems are installed, operated, and maintained safely (WorkSafe Queensland, 2020; Standards Australia & Standards New Zealand, 2018).
  • Relevant standards such as AS/NZS 3000 and AS/NZS 5033 establish requirements for safe electrical installations and solar energy systems in Australia (Standards Australia & Standards New Zealand, 2018).
  • Remote conditions can make compliance more difficult because qualified electricians, repairs, and technical support may require long travel distances (AusEco Electrical & Data, 2024).
  • Infrastructure placement must also respect Indigenous land rights and cultural heritage obligations under the Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (Qld), meaning Traditional Owners should be involved in decisions about where and how equipment is installed (Environmental Defenders Office [EDO], 2024; Queensland Government, 2025).
  • Indigenous Data Sovereignty principles further require that energy monitoring data is collected, stored, accessed, and managed in ways that maintain Lama Lama community control and consent (Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies [AIATSIS], 2019; Maiam Nayri Wingara, 2026).
  • As a result, the monitoring solution should support safe operation, culturally appropriate implementation, and transparent community-controlled data management.
E Environmental
  • Lama Lama Country experiences harsh tropical conditions including extreme heat, high humidity, cyclones, flooding, and intense wet-season rainfall, placing significant stress on off-grid energy infrastructure (Bureau of Meteorology & CSIRO, 2019).
  • Wet-season flooding and storm damage can isolate remote areas such as Port Stewart by restricting road access and delaying maintenance or emergency repairs (Queensland Reconstruction Authority, 2022).
  • High moisture, salt exposure, and severe weather conditions can accelerate corrosion and reduce the lifespan and reliability of electrical components and monitoring equipment (Engineers Without Borders Australia [EWB], 2026d; Mahmud & Roy, 2025).
  • Environmental conditions also make fault detection more difficult because technical support and replacement parts may not be immediately accessible during extreme weather events (Mahmud & Roy, 2025).
  • As a result, monitoring and fault diagnosis systems for Lama Lama Country should be weather-resistant, durable, low-maintenance, and capable of identifying faults early before they develop into major system failures.
02 Stakeholder Mapping

Whose voice matters,
and where it sits.

Conducting a rainbow stakeholder analysis helped us understand the different levels of influence, interest and responsibility across all groups involved in this project. We considered each stakeholder in terms of the PESTLE factors, gaining further insight into where their influence sits in funding, economic pressures, legal requirements and social and environmental responsibilities.

This combined approach clarified how different stakeholders impact the constraints and success of the project, and guided how we prioritised stakeholder needs in the design.

03 User Journey Map

A day in the life of a Ranger.

Mapping the Ranger's experience from system setup through fault diagnosis and repair revealed where the current process succeeds, where users feel lost, and where small design improvements would compound into significant operational gains. Each pain point informed a corresponding design requirement.
