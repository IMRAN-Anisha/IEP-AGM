I. Stage 01 · Listening & learning

Understanding Country,
people and power.

Before designing anything, we listened. We mapped the political, economic, social, technological, legal and environmental forces that shape life on Lama Lama Country — and the people whose decisions, knowledge and care make our work possible.

01 PESTLE Analysis

Six lenses on a complex system.

Desktop studies using the PESTLE framework helped us analyse the broader context influencing this project, providing multi-perspective insights. They highlighted how political structures, economic pressures, cultural responsibilities, technological limitations, legal requirements and environmental conditions all influence the direction of the design process and place constraints on the design development. Considering these factors — alongside the stakeholders involved — informs the purpose of our design to suit Rangers (our primary users) and other groups impacted by implementation.

P Political
  • Infrastructure inequality due to limited funding provided to off-grid communities for improvements.
  • Minimal investment in improved monitoring systems.
E Economic
  • High costs for transport to and from the remote location, including shipment of materials, technician callouts, high material costs and expensive maintenance procedures.
  • Funding limitations across the energy lifecycle.
S Social
  • Continuously striving for community autonomy.
  • Respect shown towards Lama Lama Peoples' cultural values and responsibilities.
  • The Aboriginal Cultural Heritage Act 2003 (QLD) provides effective recognition, protection and conservation of Aboriginal cultural heritage in Queensland, establishing a statutory duty of care for all land users.
T Technological
  • Remote location means low bandwidth and intermittent connectivity.
  • Disadvantages in education lead to lack of technical knowledge.
  • Limited funding and remote location means lack of technical equipment and services.
L Legal
  • Any system produced must adhere to relevant policies and legislation implemented in Queensland.
  • Electrical Safety Act 2002 (QLD) — establishes legal duties, licencing requirements and safety obligations to prevent electrical hazards.
  • Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (QLD) — comprehensive framework for ensuring health, safety and welfare in a workplace.
  • Privacy Act 1988 — regulates the collection, use, storage and disclosure of personal information; energy management may require storage of community usage patterns.
E Environmental
  • Harsh climate — extreme heat and humidity, flooding, cyclones, dust and pests.
  • Severe seasonal changes lead to wet-season isolation.
02 Stakeholder Mapping

Whose voice matters,
and where it sits.

Conducting a rainbow stakeholder analysis helped us understand the different levels of influence, interest and responsibility across all groups involved in this project. We considered each stakeholder in terms of the PESTLE factors, gaining further insight into where their influence sits in funding, economic pressures, legal requirements and social and environmental responsibilities. This combined approach clarified how different stakeholders impact the constraints and success of the project, and guided how we prioritised stakeholder needs in the design.

03 User Journey Map

A day in the life of a Ranger.

Mapping the Ranger's experience from system setup through fault diagnosis and repair revealed where the current process succeeds, where users feel lost, and where small design improvements would compound into significant operational gains. Each pain point informed a corresponding design requirement.
