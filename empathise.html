I. Stage 01 · Listening & learning

Understanding Country,
people and power.

Before designing anything, we listened. We mapped the political, economic, social, technological, legal and environmental forces that shape life on Lama Lama Country — and the people whose decisions, knowledge and care make our work possible.

01 PESTLE Analysis

Six lenses on a complex system.

Desktop studies using the PESTLE framework helped us analyse the broader context influencing this project by providing multi-perspective insights. These studies showed that political structures, economic pressures, cultural responsibilities, technological limitations, legal requirements, and environmental conditions all influence the direction of the design process and place constraints on design development (Queensland Competition Authority, 2025; Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water, 2024; Engineers Without Borders Australia, 2024; WorkSafe Queensland, 2020; Bureau of Meteorology & Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation, 2019). Considering these factors alongside the stakeholders involved helps define the purpose of our design, ensuring it is suitable for Rangers as the primary users while also considering other groups affected by implementation.

P Political
  • Lama Lama Country’s energy access is shaped by political and infrastructure limits because the area relies mainly on stand-alone power systems rather than the central electricity grid. This makes energy monitoring harder to upgrade, especially when existing systems have limited capacity and ongoing maintenance costs (Australian Energy Market Commission, 2019; Commonwealth of Australia, 2024; Mahmud & Roy, 2025). Queensland’s energy support, such as the Uniform Tariff Policy, mainly benefits grid-connected users, leaving off-grid communities with less long-term support for fuel, maintenance, and new monitoring infrastructure (Queensland Competition Authority, 2025). For Lama Lama, any monitoring and fault diagnosis solution must therefore be low-cost, locally manageable, and able to work independently from large-scale government infrastructure.
E Economic
  • Port Stewart’s energy monitoring design is shaped by economic limits, remote access, and the need for simple local operation. Existing Bushlight Energy Management Units use clear indicators such as lights, meters, and timers to help the community understand available power and manage battery use with little technical training (Centre for Appropriate Technology, 2008; Martire, 2020). Because Port Stewart has limited internet access, high transport and repair costs, and limited local technical support, advanced monitoring systems may be difficult to maintain (Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water, 2024; Mahmud & Roy, 2025). For Lama Lama, the monitoring solution should therefore be affordable, durable, easy to understand, and able to operate locally with minimal training.
S Social
  • Lama Lama’s fault diagnosis system needs to be culturally appropriate, simple to use, and designed around the community’s real conditions. Because reliable energy supports communication, education, healthcare, employment, and overall wellbeing, faults can have wider impacts beyond the power system itself (Centre for Appropriate Technology, n.d.; Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2026). A co-designed approach is important because Lama Lama Country is connected to land, waters, and Sea Country, and local knowledge from Elders, Rangers, and community members can help shape a system that fits seasonal changes, wet-season isolation, cyclones, and maintenance limits (Engineers Without Borders Australia, 2024; Yintjingga Aboriginal Corporation, n.d.). For this project, the solution should support local capability, reduce reliance on external technicians, protect community-controlled data, and help the Lama Lama community maintain energy access while caring for Country.
T Technological
  • Lama Lama Country has existing off-grid solar infrastructure at Port Stewart, including Bushlight systems with photovoltaic arrays, batteries, inverters, charge controllers, and Energy Management Units that were originally designed through community energy planning (Centre for Appropriate Technology, 2008). However, maintaining these systems is difficult because remote Indigenous communities often face limited technical support, long travel distances, and maintenance gaps when institutional support changes or fails (Heard et al., 2025; Mahmud & Roy, 2025). Fault detection is therefore important because undetected solar photovoltaic faults can reduce power generation, shorten system life, and create safety risks (El-Banby et al., 2023; Venkatakrishnan et al., 2023). For Lama Lama, a suitable solution should use simple continuous monitoring, automated alerts, and local-friendly operation so faults can be detected early and escalated for timely support.
L Legal
  • Legal and governance requirements are important for any energy monitoring system on Lama Lama Country because the system must be safe, culturally appropriate, and properly managed. Electrical monitoring equipment must follow Queensland electrical safety and work health and safety requirements, including safe installation, maintenance, and risk control for users and workers (WorkSafe Queensland, 2020; Standards Australia & Standards New Zealand, 2018). Infrastructure placement also needs to respect Lama Lama authority, Indigenous land rights, and cultural heritage obligations, meaning Traditional Owners should be involved in deciding where and how equipment is installed (Environmental Defenders Office, 2024; Queensland Government, 2025). Energy data must also be handled carefully, with community control, consent, and Indigenous Data Sovereignty guiding how monitoring information is collected, stored, accessed, and used (Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies, 2019; Maiam nayri Wingara, 2018). For this project, the system should therefore support safe electrical use, culturally respectful placement, and community-controlled data management.
E Environmental
  • Port Stewart, Lama Lama’s environment creates major challenges for off-grid energy systems because the area is exposed to tropical heat, heavy wet-season rainfall, flooding, storms, moisture, and difficult access. These conditions can damage equipment, reduce durability, and make faults harder to detect or repair when roads are closed or support is delayed (Bureau of Meteorology & Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation, 2019; Queensland Reconstruction Authority, 2022). Because of this, energy monitoring and fault diagnosis need to remain reliable during harsh conditions, not only during normal operation. For Lama Lama, the system should therefore be weather-resistant, low-maintenance, and able to detect faults early so the community and rangers can respond before small issues become serious disruptions.
02 Stakeholder Mapping

Whose voice matters,
and where it sits.

Conducting a rainbow stakeholder analysis helped us understand the different levels of influence, interest and responsibility across all groups involved in this project. We considered each stakeholder in terms of the PESTLE factors, gaining further insight into where their influence sits in funding, economic pressures, legal requirements and social and environmental responsibilities. This combined approach clarified how different stakeholders impact the constraints and success of the project, and guided how we prioritised stakeholder needs in the design.

03 User Journey Map

A day in the life of a Ranger.

Mapping the Ranger's experience from system setup through fault diagnosis and repair revealed where the current process succeeds, where users feel lost, and where small design improvements would compound into significant operational gains. Each pain point informed a corresponding design requirement.
